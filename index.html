Authority Multipliers

Seven levers that amplify your lighthouse signal

1. Specificity Over Generality

Name the exact dollar amount, the specific concept, the real scenario. "Food cost hit 38% that month" beats "costs were high."

2. Hindsight + Current Learning

"I made X mistake. Now I'm learning Y concept. Here's how they connect." This combination is unusually honest and useful.

3. Mirror, Not Advice

"If you've ever felt X, then Y might feel familiar." Let people see themselves. Don't tell them what to do.

4. Short-Paragraph Rhythm

1-3 sentences per paragraph. White space is your friend. LinkedIn readers skim first, then return to read if the rhythm pulls them in.

5. Tension & Contrast

The best posts hold two truths in tension: "I was good at leading people AND terrible at understanding my own business."

6. Strategic Commenting

3-5 thoughtful 2-sentence comments per week on CFOs, finance leaders, and CPA voices. This generates more profile views than posting.

7. The Shelf Test

"Would I be proud of this post in six months?" Lighthouse content ages well. Trendy content doesn't. If the answer is yes, post it. If not, it needs more thought.

The 4-Question Content Filter

Run every post through this before publishing

1. Is this useful?
Would a thoughtful operator, finance learner, or career-builder walk away with something worth the 90 seconds it takes to read?
2. Is this true to my voice?
Would someone who knows me personally recognize this? Does it sound like a real person or like "LinkedIn content"?
3. Is this relevant to my audience?
Does this connect to leadership, business literacy, the CPA journey, or running something?
4. Is this worth sharing even if it gets 12 likes?
If one thoughtful comment from exactly the right person is enough, post it.

What to Avoid

Red Flags
✗ Lessons framed as universal truths when they're personal observations
✗ Vulnerability as strategy — sharing struggle just for engagement
✗ Trendy formats that don't match your voice (generic carousels, hot-takes)
✗ Claiming expertise you don't yet have
✗ Posting about posting ("what it means to show up on LinkedIn...")
✗ Motivational content with no specificity
✗ Engagement bait questions unconnected to your thinking

Why This Post Worked

Pattern Analysis
1. Contrarian Hook (Line 1)

Flips an assumption: finance isn't underperforming, it's overperforming for others. Creates instant cognitive tension — the reader has to reconcile this.

2. Cinematic Moment

"I let them finish. Then I said it." — This is screenplay pacing. Short. Deliberate. Builds anticipation before the punchline. The reader is IN the room.

3. Bold Statement as Turning Point

"Your inability to manage your own function is not finance's problem to solve." — This is the line people screenshot and share. Specific, confrontational, undeniably true for the audience.

4. Repetition as Rhythm Engine

"Finance finds out... Finance is reconciling... Finance inherits..." — Parallel structure builds momentum. Each repetition hammers the thesis deeper. The reader nods harder each time.

5. The Inversion

"Those same teams sit in the same meetings wondering why finance isn't more strategic." — This is the killer irony. The very people causing the problem are questioning the victims of it.

6. Quotable Close + Open Question

The closing line is shareable on its own. The question invites personal reflection without being engagement bait — because the post already earned the right to ask.

Your Lighthouse Post Template

The structure behind every strong post

1

Open with a Specific Truth (1-2 lines)

Not a hook. Not a question designed to grab attention. A sentence that is just true, and specific enough that the right person immediately recognizes it. The goal is resonance, not reach.

Example: "Running a restaurant with no idea how to read a P&L is like driving with a map you can't interpret."
2

Short Story or Moment (3-6 lines)

One real situation. Keep it tight. What happened, what you thought at the time, and what you understand now. No need to dramatize — the specificity does the work.

3

The Applicable Takeaway (2-3 lines)

What does this mean for someone else? Not advice — more like a mirror. "If you've ever [X], then [Y] might feel familiar." Or simply: "Here's the thing I'd do differently."

4

Close with a Thought or Question (1 line)

Optional. A strong final sentence that doesn't ask anything is better than a hollow question. Only ask when it's genuine and earned by the post.

The Rule

Never dark for more than 3 weeks. Disappearing for a month means starting from a lower baseline. A simple reappearance post — honest about where you've been — resets the rhythm.

Commenting Strategy

High-ROI Activity

Who to Comment On

CFOs, finance leaders, CPA voices, operators, career-changers, founders sharing lessons — people in or adjacent to your niche.

How to Comment

Two sentences that show you actually read the post. Add a personal connection or extend their point. A two-sentence comment > ten emoji reactions.

Connection Requests

1-2 per week. Always include a short personal note referencing what you commented on. Never a generic "I'd like to add you."

Voice & Tone Guidelines

How you should sound — and how you shouldn't

Sound Like This

Do
Thoughtful — you've sat with this before sharing it
Credible — specific enough to be believed
Warm — you care about the reader's situation
Self-aware — you know what you don't know yet
Practical — there's something usable here
Clear — a 12-year-old could follow the logic
Humble — you're on the journey, not above it

Not Like This

Don't
Reactive — posting something because it just happened
Vague — so general it could apply to anyone
Transactional — the post is really about your metrics
Overconfident — claiming expertise you're still building
Abstract — inspiring but not actionable
Jargon-heavy — using finance terms to signal authority
Preachy — telling people what they should do

A Note on Learning Out Loud

When writing about accounting or finance topics you're currently studying, the most trustworthy framing is always:

"Here's what I'm learning, here's what it made me realize, and here's why I think it matters for people in situations like mine."

You don't need to be the expert. You need to be the honest student with real-world context. That's actually harder to find on LinkedIn — and more valuable.
